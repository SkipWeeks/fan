HISTORY OF PERCIA CORNELIA GROVER BUNNELL
Written by herself at Provo, Utah 13 February 1881

I, Percia Cornelia Grover, was born the 27th Dec. 1841 in Nauvoo, Illinois. My father was a member of the High Council at the time that plural marriage was revealed to the church, and was called upon to obey the law. He had three wives sealed to him, besides my mother, in the Temple in Nauvoo.

In Feb. 1845, he was counciled with a company of brethern, to start for the west, which he did, taking with him his family. We suffered much with the inclemency of the weather. Our team, being insufficient to take all his family, after traveling two hundred and fifty miles, my mother returned to Nauvoo, taking me with her.

We came out the following September with her father and mother. We suffered much and many fell by the way. Grandpa Nickerson, being in years could not stand the hardships of the journey and fell sick. So we were obliged to stop for the winter on the Platte River, where he died, leaving us without anyone to protect us.

My mother and grandmother returned back to their friends and stayed one year. My great-grandmother died while they were there, aged 94 years. My mother and grandmother then started for the Valley of the Salt Lake. We stopped in Fort Des Moines one winter.

My mother took in washings to get provisions to come to the valley. When we came to Cainesville in the spring, we met with a former friend of my father, from Utah, who had some money belonging to my father. He helped us to get a team to come on to the valley. We had one yoke of oxen and a yoke of cows. We were three months on the way. We were five in the family: mother, grandmother, two brothers and myself.

My oldest brother, twelve years old, with the help of mother drove the team. We all walked most of the way. We arrived in Salt Lake City, Sept. 27, 1850. Being too late to put up hay, our stock all died but one. Mother kept school in a small room she rented in Salt Lake City. We lived in the same room and got through the winter without much suffering.

In the fall of 1852, we moved to Provo, where I have lived ever since. I have seen many trials and suffering among the saints but feel to thank the Lord that I am counted worthy to fill a position in the midst of his people.

On Sept 19, 1856, I married to Stephen I. Bunnell. Trusting that my posterity may live faithful to the end and help to bear testimony of the great work which their parents have suffered much to help in these the latter-days and give to themselves salvation in the celestial kingdom of Jesus Christ. I will now sign myself your affectionate mother and sister in the Gospel of our Savior, Jesus Christ,

Signed: Percia Cornelia Bunnell

Note: In a Family History written by L. Lamond Bunnell, 1972, in his 78th year, and from the recollections of Leona Bunnell Nuttall, both children of Leslie L. and B. J. Bunnell, and therefore grandchildren of Stephen Ithamer and Percia C. G. Bunnell, the following has been added:

Grandma (Percia C. Grover) was the daughter of Thomas Grover and Eliza Nickerson (Hubbard). Both Thomas and Eliza had been married before and both had lost their mates. L. Lamond Bunnell recalls that: Grandma Percia told that when they were crossing the plains, they were held up one whole day because the buffalo were stampeding. It started early in the morning before they had assembled their wagons for the morning start. They were camped in a small grove of trees, one of the few times they had a campsite like that. They first heard a noise like thunder and then saw a cloud of dust. They got the cattle in tight to the camp and waited. They were afraid that the buffalo might come right through the camp but when they were near the grove, they spread out and went around the camp on both sides. They lost a day's journey on account of the stampede.

Grandma was an exceptionally good cook. Many of the Church Authorities have eaten at her home. I remember once when Wilford Woodruff and Lorenzo Snow drove out from Provo in an old dog cart and spent a couple of hours. I was at Grandma's. I think it was when my younger sister Leona was born and Grandpa was babysitting me to keep me out of the way. When the brethren left, they shook hands with me and patted me on the head and told me I was a good boy. I have never forgotten the incident.

Grandma was famous for her gooseberry pie. Everyone who ever tasted it wanted the recipe and she was always willing to oblige. She would tell all about how to make it with all the ingredients named except the sugar. When she would come to that part, she always inched up to the edge of her chair, then, lifting her left hand up, she would say, " and now you come to the sugar..." and then she would inch up a bit further and say, "You put in all your conscience will let you and then shut your eyes and throw in three double hands full." It always got a big chuckle.

Grandma was very proud, loyal, industrious, and dignified and had a host of friends. She was very strong willed and had a very quick temper. When she got mad. ...look out: She abhorred laziness. Leona Bunnell Nuttall recalls that: After coming to Utah, Grandmother's mother (Eliza Nickerson Hubbard Grover) taught school to make a living for them even though they lived in only one room at the time.

Her grandmother, Eliza, would shear the sheep, prepare the wool for spinning, then weave the cloth and make it into clothing. When they wanted to dye the cloth, they used bark and berries from the trees and shrubs that grew around the area. Leona remembers a quilt made from pieces from some of the worn-out clothes made from the dyed cloth, and even though the quilt was old, the colors were still bright and beautiful.

Grandma Percia told me about the time her mother (Eliza) made a suit of clothes for a man. She made a mistake and cut the pants so that they buttoned on the wrong side. As she had no more material when she discovered her mistake, she was at a loss to know what to do but finally went ahead and made them up. She told the man that she had made them that way on purpose, because she thought it would be better for him as he was left-handed. The man was very pleased and flattered that she had been so thoughtful.

Because I (Leona) have always been slow and lazy (Grandma Percia had no patience for those traits in anyone) my memories and impressions of Grandma in my early life are not always of the most complimentary kind. She was an extremely hard worker herself. She spent a lot of time at our home and did a lot to help. I'm not sure that either Mother (Ellen B.J. Bunnell) or I appreciated it and it has left me with a fear of doing the wrong thing in the wrong way, when I want to help someone.

As I got older, I learned to appreciate and admire her more. I do appreciate her real desire for service and her faith and determination. Although many things happened to try her faith, she never faltered. Once when I dared to criticize a step one of the Church Authorities had taken, she straightened me out good by telling me that with all the members of the church praying for the Lord to guide the Authorities in their decisions, that their counsel could not be wrong.

I (Leona) remember her telling me how many of the people of Provo used to drive out for her dinners. She said that the reason her food tasted better than other cooks was because she always had and used plenty of good thick cream in most of her recipes. She was very proud of her friends among the "best" (as she called them) people of Provo: the Knights, the Twelves, the Smoots, and many others.

However, she was also modest. She told me once that the only reason she was on the Stake Board of the Relief Society was because she had a horse and buggy to haul the women who didn't have a horse and buggy around. In her later years, after she and Grandpa (Steven Ithamer Bunnell) were divorced...she didn't believe in polygamy and saw what polygamy did to her own mother's life...she lived alone.

She built a small two room home south of Lars Jacobsen's home. She had a barn for Old Jim, her horse. She had Old Jim barn-broken. He always waited until Grandma came out in the morning to take him out of the barn to a corner of his yard. He just never dirtied the barn for her to have to shovel out. Grandma had a carriage that was called a phaeton which she used for transportation. It did not have sides but was low slung which made it easier for her to get in and out of.

Old Jim was a very good buggy horse...one of the best trotting horses in the area. He was one of the racing horses that her son, Steve, used to use but was now too old for him. Many people tried to "pass" Grandma and Old Jim but they were never successful. All Grandma had to do was to say, "Come on Jim" and his back would drop as he reached out into his fast stride and he left his contender behind him. Many times we would see a cloud of dust down the road and would say, "Wonder who Grandma is racing now?" Sure enough, here would come Grandma sitting right on the edge of the front seat, talking to Old Jim. I never knew of him being beaten in one of these races. He always made Grandma proud of him.

Much of this was when she was in her late 70's. In the fall, when [sugar] beet digging time arrived, Grandma used to carry a stick with a nail in the end in the buggy with her. Quite often one of the wagons loaded with beets, driving to the factory to unload, would drop off a beet alongside the road. Old Jim loved to eat the beets. When he would see one, he would stop and Grandma would spear the beet with stick and nail and put it in the buggy for that evening's dessert for Old Jim.

Grandma Percia was one of the few women who could speak the Indian dialect. Lamond remembers one 24th of July Celebration in Provo, when each ward in Utah Stake had to represent a certain phase of Pioneer life. Lakeview had to be the Indians. Lamond's mother [Ellen] made him an Indian outfit out of new burlap: “pants with fringe and a jacket, and a quiver for my arrows. One of the neighbors, Art Taylor, had a real Indian mare, a small typical Indian pony that he let me ride in the parade. As we passed Grandma Percia's place in Provo, she was outside by the fence. She treated us as real Indians and talked Indian talk to us as long as we were within hearing distance. I was so proud of her accomplishment in being able to speak like an Indian."

Grandma died the second of February 1924 at Provo, Utah.
