HIGH SCHOOL

I attended Orem High School, located at approximately 250 South 400 East in Orem. One of the definite highlights of my sophomore year was that I learned to drive. The day I went to take my driving test it was a cold March day, I’m sure, on or about my birthday. We went down to Provo to the DMV office. I took the test, and I got to drive home. What a great feeling it was to be able to drive, legally. I loved it.

 

My Sophomore Year

 

I can’t think of very many other things as satisfying as the feeling I felt the day that my parents said that I could take the car, all my myself, alone. What a feeling of power and freedom that was. In spite of the fact that I knew I couldn’t or at least shouldn’t go very far, it was incredibly exhilarating to be out and on my own - trusted and empowered to go and do what I wanted or needed to do, just like an adult.

My Most Embarrassing Moment

Everybody has one and I am no different. I have my most embarrassing moment and here it is. When I was a Sophomore in high school I went on a double date with my friend, Boyd Nelson. He took his girlfriend and I asked a girl named Carol Johnson from Lindon. She and I dated for a few months during my tenth grade year. We decided to go hiking in Provo canyon, near a place called Upper Falls. This area is a few hundred feet up the river from Rotary Park, and it was one of the places with a bridge that crossed the Provo River. We all piled into Boyd’s Volkswagen bug and took off up the canyon. We got to the Upper Falls, parked the car, crossed the bridge and all started climbing up a pretty steep incline.

 I recall getting up the hill about two hundred feet, when I had a sudden and devastating attack of diarrhea, down both legs, without warning. As one can imagine, I was quite unprepared for this. I felt that I had no other choice, but to turn on my heels and bolt down the hill, without any explanation, hoping that everyone would follow me down, thinking that I had just gotten some stroke of inspiration to do something else really fun - or something??? Actually, I didn’t know or really care what they did, I just didn’t want to confront them with such a messy and unpleasant surprise.

 When I got to the bottom of the hill, instead of crossing the bridge, I kept running down, right into the middle of the river. I knew that I couldn’t ride home the way I was, and I couldn’t face any of my friends in the condition I was, either. I knew I had to do something, quickly, to eliminate my extra smelly baggage. I reasoned that I could slosh around enough in the river to loosen the debris inside my pants, if I was lucky.

 My friends must have thought that I was crazy. They chased me down the mountain and stared in horror as I jumped into the rushing water and started tromping and jumping up and down through the icy water. They began to holler and beg me to get out of the water. I couldn’t get out until I believed that I had rinsed enough of my problem away to be indiscernible to my friends. I continued to tromp and jump up and down, hoping to flush out the remaining evidence of my problem. Finally, I felt that it would be safe to climb out as they continued to plead with me to do. I sloshed up the rocky side of the river to a rather amazed group of kids.

 I gave them some kind of lame excuse and they suggested that we go home, so that I could get warm and a change of clothing. I gladly agreed, but was unwilling to get into Boyd’s car wearing my wet stinking jeans. I suggested that I stand on the rear bumper of his car, and hold onto his ski rack, attached to the back window. They all objected but I insisted.

 I can only guess what they must have talked about, as we slowly drove home, with them inside, thinking that I would fall off any minute into traffic, and me hanging on stubbornly to the back of Boyd’s VW bug in the wind. I think that we arranged to drop Carol off in Lindon on the way to my house. Boyd dropped me off at my home and my most embarrassing moment was finally over. I, later, told Boyd what had happened but I never told the girls.
