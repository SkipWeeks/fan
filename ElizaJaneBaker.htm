History of Eliza Jane Baker Peay
Born 1834
Died 1915

Biography written by her daughter, Rosetta Pricilla Peay Wride

My mother, Eliza Jane Baker Peay was born in Poole, Dorchester, England May 18, 1834. Her mother died when she was eleven years old, so she went out as a nurse- maid in families where a girl was needed to care for children. She was kept busy so did not get to school any after that, but she was very good at figures, spelling and reading. She loved to read and took great pleasure in her books and magazines. After her father joined the church, he was anxious to go to Utah; but as he was unable to do so at the time, he decided to send mother who was then eighteen. She was put in the charge of a man and wife who were sailing with a group of saints. My father, Francis Peay was born in Sussex, England September 23, 1825. As a boy of seven, he was apprenticed out to a ship builder. This became his trade and in young manhood he was married to Prudence Collyer. They had two little sons. It was about this time that my mother first became acquainted with my father and his wife. She said she used to see this fine looking well-dressed man and his wife and sons go to chapel and admired him thinking that she would be proud to have a husband such as he. His happiness with his wife and sons were very short, for the family took scarlet fever and his wife and both sons died within a few days of each other, my father being ill at the time. In the year 1852 father sent his mother, a sister and two brothers to America, remaining behind to earn money for his journey over. The following year when he arrived in America he was much disappointed and very sad to learn his mother had died. It happened that the ship that Mother sailed on was the same one on which Francis Peay, my Father, was sailing. Their acquaintance was renewed and each being lonely there was a bond between them, which soon ripened into love, and they were married on board ship by Elder Jacob Gates. They were on board ship for eleven weeks. When they landed they had to leave many of their possessions, as there was not room in the wagons of the company they came to Utah with. They had many hardships along with the rest of the company, among who were Brother Charles Savage, Brother William Eddington and other prominent Utah pioneers. I have heard mother say she walked most of the way across the plains. They came to Provo and took up a homestead in the western part of what is now known as Provo City, Utah. During the Indian trouble, they had to move into the Fort, but as soon as the trouble was settled, they went back to the farm; Father planted peach pits all around the outside of the farm, and when they grew up, mother and we girls gathered the delicious fruit and dried enough to buy our first stove. The first home father built on the farm was built with adobe walls, the adobe being made by mother, the children and himself; and the roof was thatched with rushes and hauled from the shores of Utah Lake. Mother made lye from wood ashes in a leach or large barrel with a tap at the bottom to allow the lye to drain. From the lye she made soap, using grease or fat for the base ingredient. She also made the candles. She made dye from dogwood berries and made medicine from roots, herbs and berries of different kinds. Onion oil she used as medicine, and to check vomiting she used the dried and grated chicken gizzard skins. They had their socials and good times in the form of corn husking bees, ground cherry shelling, ect. Our farm was an ideal place for picnics. A large creek with wild roses growing on its banks flowed through the farm and the Sunday School used to go there for their outings. Father was interested in promoting mining and did much prospecting and hauling supplies to Tintic district for the miners. For years mother churned butter and packed it in ten pound buckets and shipped it to Salt Lake twice a week. She walked long distances to carry it to the depot. She also delivered butter and eggs to regular customers in Provo, walking several miles in the process. She was a great lover of flowers and raised many kinds both in the garden and in her home. Mother and Father had twelve children, six boys and six girls, ten living to maturity. We children loved to sit on the porch, in the evenings and have mother and father sing to us. They had good voices and were indeed lovers of music. Father led the choir at Provo for several years. He died at the age of 79 and mother at 81 years. Additional information about Eliza Jane Baker: Eliza Jane was baptized April 7, 1851. She was the oldest child in her family. She had two brothers and three step- brothers and two step- sisters. Lilly, the youngest daughter of Eliza spoke of a swing they had, on their farm in Provo, that they could go back and forth across the stream of water that flowed by their house. She said they had a big cellar, big enough to drive a wagon into. They had many kinds of fruit and vegetables to store in it. They also raised sheep as she told of carding the wool to make quilts. He mother would read to the family at mealtimes and this love of reading carried over to her children. Whenever Lily spoke of her parents it was always with love and respect. Eliza Jane Baker spent the last years of her life with her daughter, Prudence, in Vineyard. She was completely helpless for many years and bed-ridden. Her daughter lovingly cared for her to the end. She died August 7, 1915 in Vineyard, Utah, at the home of her daughter. She was a refined, cultured, intelligent, hardworking, and industrious. She left a wonderful posterity and we owe her a great debt of gratitude.
