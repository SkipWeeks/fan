My Magnificent Mother
Bertha Augusta Margarita Larsen Weeks

By Clyde E. Weeks, Jr.


Sixty-five years is a very long time for a person to be thinking about his mother. But when a mother dies at a young age, those children she leaves behind are faced with the reality of living the rest of their lives without the benefit of their dear mother’s mortal presence and companionship.

Moreover, those spouses and children, who live out their own mortal lives, over a good many years, are almost always prone to wonder how their own lives might have taken different paths, had their mothers, early in their young lives, not succumbed to the hazards and infirmities to which flesh is subject on this planet Earth.

History happens to everyone, but most people are so busy living their lives of quiet desperation, that the significant, as well as insignificant events of their lives, are never recorded, so that generations who come after them may know the details of their lives.

Fortunate, indeed, are the relatively few people, who leave behind them some kind of record, so that their progeny can learn of the circumstances, people and events, which have shaped their lives. Some of the world’s most notable people have even inspired others to see the need to record elements of their lives, which those who follow, may later read and become either illuminated or incredulous.

SHE KEPT A PERSONAL DIARY
Such a one was Bertha Augusta Margarita Larsen, who, in her relatively young life, kept a personal diary over a two-year period, in which she recorded her thoughts, her aspirations and activities, during the time she was training to become a registered nurse at Dr. W.H. Groves Latter-Day Saints Hospital in Salt Lake City.





But, whence came this enterprising young woman, determined to follow in the footsteps of one of her heroines in history: Florence Nightingale, storied advocate of enlightened nursing, during her valiant nursing service in the Crimean War, as well as during her many years of service as Superintendent of Nursing in England?

Bertha Augusta Margarita Larsen was the daughter of immigrants from Scandinavia, following the lure of a new religion and a new life in the United States of America.

Bertha’s mother, Inga Augusta Himmerman, was born August 27, 1869, in Asa, Torp, Vesternorrland, Sweden. She was born just about 21 years after Mormon pioneers, members of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, entered Salt Lake Valley on July 24, 1847.

While Inga Himmerman was a young adult in Sweden, she met two proselytizing missionaries from The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, who had traveled all the way from the American West to Scandinavia to seek converts to their church. Fascinated by their story of an American youth, Joseph Smith Jr., she learned that he had seen God, and was told by him, that he should be God’s instrument in restoring Christ’s original church. Inga Himmerman embraced the new church, headquartered in Salt Lake City, and resolved to find a way to join the main body of the Church in America.

MEMBERS OF LDS CHURCH
Meanwhile, young Inga met a young Swedish compatriot, named Lars Ostlund. They married, became the parents of two sons: Dean and Bert Ostlund. The couple immigrated to the United States to join the main body of the church, and were, later, divorced.

Some time after the divorce, Inga met an older man from Denmark in Utah, fifteen years her senior, who also had become acquainted with the new American church and had immigrated to Utah. Christian Larsen had been born November 4, 1854, in Kamstrup, Vor Feve, Copenhagen, Denmark. After a short courtship, Christian Larsen and Inga Himmerman were married and lived for several years in northern Utah, near Bear Lake.

In 1899 the Christian and Inga Larsen family joined a company of 32 other pioneer families, who were called by Brigham Young to leave Sevier County and travel to settle some of the land around Cardston, Alberta, Canada. Led by Bishop Theodore Brandley, the hardy company of LDS Saints set out for that distant land, across the border and into the newly-being-settled country of Canada.

Waiting for them at the end of their long journey would be thousands of acres of undeveloped land, from which they were expected to wrench a living for their families and establish a thriving community of stalwart believers in the Gospel of Jesus Christ, as promulgated by The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints.

With faith in every footstep, Christian and Inga Larsen were assigned their own homestead plot, as they set out to arrange some kind of temporary shelter there, to serve until a proper home could be built to house their family.

HEROIC COOPERATIVE EFFORTS
Agriculture and its appurtenant occupations and labors were the only options available to the new Canadians. Those people, who had previously practiced farming skills in other settings around the world, had a decided advantage over those whose talents lay in other directions. However, the new settlers took an inventory of their assorted skills and talents and through heroic cooperative efforts, homes were built, farms were developed, schools were organized and towns were established, around which their lives would revolve for generations to come.

Central to each of the small towns, established by these Mormon pioneers, was a chapel of quite humble circumstances, where the Latter-day Saints could hold their church meetings, and socialize.

Sterling, Alberta, Canada, became the home of Christian and Inga Larsen. The Larsens cleared the lands to which they were assigned, and taught their five sons and one daughter the value of work.

On December 8, 1900, Christian Wilford Larsen was born--the second boy to be born in that area.

Bertha Augusta Margarita Larsen, the Larsen’s only daughter, was born April 11, 1903. A son, Seth, was born in 1907, and Grant was born in 1912.

Life in Alberta, Canada, during the first half of the Twentieth Century was challenging for most of its citizens. Cultivating the land in one way or another occupied the time and efforts of its residents, and for the Christian Larsen family, their agrarian society challenged every member to participate in an environment, where the role of “good neighbor” was well-understood.

STRONG SUPPORT FOR EDUCATION
Although constantly challenged by the growing need for helping hands to plant and harvest the fields, the Alberta schools received strong support from the parents of the children, who attended school and strove to succeed. Bertha Larsen was one of those, who took her education very seriously, realizing how important it would surely become later in her life.

With the children of Sterling being educated and graded monthly in 12 different subjects, punctuality and regular attendance were given the highest priority in every school. In the Galt School District public schools, the Christian Larsen children were both encouraged and helped by their parents to succeed in school.

In each of their 12 school classes, students were graded thusly: A, Excellent; B, Good; C, Fair; D, Poor; and E, Bad.

The fact that young Bertha Larsen was an outstanding student in school, is shown by this copy of her Monthly Report in Grade VII for 1916. Although her “Deportment” was graded “B” for each of the four months, her ranking in class fluctuated between first and second place.

The Christian Larsen home was a typical Mormon home, with the close-knit family kneeling for family prayers at the dining table at every meal. Young Bertha had a special place in the hearts of her five brothers, in spite of the occasional practical jokes she played on them.

Even as a young child, Bertha was her mother’s willing ‘right hand,’ said her brothers. She would help her mother prepare food and wash the dishes, while making note of her mother’s recipes for favorite dishes, which her family enjoyed. She also learned to sew clothing for herself, as well as for her brothers.
An avid reader, Bertha was fond of perusing the works of Charles Dickens, especially “A Christmas Carol” as well as historical accounts of the development of western America. She was also captivated by the exploits of Florence Nightingale, who had become a legend in her own time through her exploits in the Crimean War.

While visiting the gardens at Embley, England, Florence had received what she described as her ‘calling’. Florence said she heard the voice of God, calling her to do his work, but at that time, she had no idea what that work would become.
From a well-to-do British family, Florence developed an interest in the social questions of her day, made visits to the homes of the sick in local villages, and began to investigate hospitals and the roles of nurses in British society. She became a nurse, herself, and served as a shining light for women, in her successful efforts to see the career of nursing become a respected profession for women.

By 1917 Bertha Larsen’s half-brothers, Gustav and Einer Ostlund had gained their maturity, leaving their step-father’s and mother’s modest home to go out on their own. Wilford Larsen was seventeen, and Bertha was fourteen years old. Seth Larsen was ten years old, while Grant Larsen was eight years old.

THEIR WORLD CHANGED DRASTICALLY
Meanwhile, the Larsen’s world was about to change, more drastically than they could have imagined.

The Great Epidemic, also known as the ‘Influenza Epidemic of 1918-1919,’ caused the deaths of 675,000 Americans between September 1918 and June 1919. But it was also a plague that affected hundreds of thousands of people all over the earth. It caused many more deaths than any of the other epidemics which had preceded it.

The epidemic was reported to have come in two waves. The first wave, in the spring of 1918, took far less than the second. The United States and the rest of the world had been exposed to such epidemics in the past, but never at such a severe cost in human life.

In Canada the worldwide influenza epidemic manifest itself, at least a year earlier than in the United States. In the spring of 1917 the specter of influenza visited the small town of Sterling, Alberta, Canada, as well as a number of other Canadian communities. People were getting sick. People were dying. But no one knew at that time how immense the threat of that plague constituted to the rest of the world.

Early in March, 1917, in Sterling, Alberta, Canada, 64-year-old Christian Larsen was not feeling well. His body ached severely. His fever was high and he suffered from chills. Nothing seemed to relieve his illness.

His wife, Inga, made him as comfortable as possible during his terminal illness, and doctors were baffled as to what possible treatment might thwart the disease. Christian Larsen, finally, died March 22, 1917. His family was heartbroken.

At that time, most doctors cited ‘pneumonia’ on the death certificates of those who succumbed, since flu came first and weakened the resistance of the sick; then pneumonia followed and was the eventual cause of death for most people thus afflicted.

DOUBLE TRAGEDY FOR FAMILY
And yet, the tragedy in the Larsen family was doubled seventeen days later, when the virulent disease also took Inga Larsen, the mother of the six children, on April 9, 1917.

Of course, the Christian Larsen household was not the only family to be devastated by the incipient onslaught of the influenza epidemic in their rustic community, but the loss of both the parents in such a short time frame presented a knotty problem of major proportions: where would the under-age orphans live, and who would be responsible for their care and rearing to adulthood?

However, the Larsen family had lived in Canada for seventeen years. They had made many friends and had been active in the Sterling Ward of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints. The under-age orphaned children--Wilford, Bertha, Seth and Grant-- were taken into the homes of four separate Mormon foster families.

Merlin and Alice Steed, who already had nine children of their own, seven boys and two girls, invited seventeen-year-old Wilford to join their family.

He lived with them from the time he was seventeen, until he was called on a proselytizing LDS mission to the Northern States in Chicago, when he was nineteen.

Fourteen-year-old Bertha Larsen took eight-year-old Grant to nearby Tabor to live with the Peter Larsens (no relation), former residents of Sterling. She returned and lived a short time with Lon and Hulda Nelson. Then she went to live with her friend, Zula Kimball’s family and attended the Knight Academy.
Ten-year-old Seth Larsen was welcomed into the family of Bishop Arthur E. Fawns.

Fortunately, the four children lived nearby each other in their new family settings and were able to maintain their close family ties.

BERTHA WANTED TO BE A NURSE
Meanwhile, Bertha’s admiration for Florence Nightingale continued. Florence had died at home in England at the age of 90 on August 13, 1910. In her old age she had received many honors, including Great Britain’s Order of Merit and the Royal Red Cross.

As she completed her studies at the Knight Academy, Bertha was strongly impressed with the idea that she, too, might undertake a nursing career and become a most productive and useful member of society.

Florence Nightingale’s farsighted nursing reforms had widely influenced the nature of modern health care, and her writings continued to be a resource for nurses, health managers and planners.

Encouraged in her aspirations by her friends and siblings, Bertha Larsen prepared to leave Sterling, Alberta, Canada, and move to Salt Lake City, Utah, planning to enroll for classes at the relatively new Dr. W.H. Groves Latter-day Saints’ Hospital Training School.

In 1921 Wilford Larsen returned to Sterling from his two-year mission in Chicago. His fourteen-year-old brother, Seth, was still living with the Arthur Fawns family, and twelve-year-old Grant, was then living with Perry and May Burke in Sterling.

Eighteen-year-old Bertha Larsen was ready to spread her wings and pursue her dream of becoming a registered nurse. She kissed her brothers ‘good-by’ and set out for Salt Lake City, full of hope and faith that life had many good things to offer her in the big city.

DR. W.H. GROVES LATTER-DAY SAINTS HOSPITAL
Salt Lake City in 1921, the center of Mormonism, was a thriving small metropolis in Utah—the Beehive State--which had developed in three-quarters of a century from a sagebrush ridden valley on the shores of the Great Salt Lake to the fulfillment of Biblical prophecy that the desert shall “rejoice and blossom as the rose.”

Upon her arrival in Salt Lake City, Bertha excitedly visited the center of town, where Temple Square contained the LDS temple and tabernacle, and the statue of Mormon Church President Brigham Young was seen, welcoming new-comers to the city.

Four blocks to the north of Brigham Young’s statue, and four blocks to the east, Bertha discovered the Dr. W.H. Groves Latter-day Saints Hospital and its School of Nursing. Bertha was overwhelmed to see the magnificent towered brick structure, topped by the colorful stars and stripes, which was the Flag of the United States of America.

Rows of thriving trees surrounded the hospital and attractively set off the magnificent hospital, located in the Avenues of Salt Lake City.
As she entered the large medical facility, there in the lobby, she saw on a wall this large plaque, which reinforced, once again, her determination to follow the bright path, inspired by her spiritual mentor, Florence Nightingale:

      

          
The Dr. W.H. Groves LDS Hospital in Salt Lake City, owned by the LDS Church, was established in 1905. It was largely funded through a bequest of W.H. Groves, an LDS dentist, who had come to Utah from Nottingham, England. The hospital, a five-story complex with eighty beds, was equipped with up-to-date medical equipment and innovations, including an elevator and a nurse-calling system.

Located nearby the hospital was the newly-constructed Nurses Home, which would become the home to Bertha Larsen, as a student at the hospital’s School of Nursing. The first class of graduating nurses from the School of Nursing was in 1906.

Bertha Larsen introduced herself to Lucy P. Pocock, R.N., Superintendent of Nurses, and told her how excited she was to be accepted for admission to the Hospital Training School.

As Bertha reviewed the list of faculty members at the school, she was mightily impressed with breadth of the curriculum and those, who would be teaching her everything she needed to know about nursing.

Beginning her nursing classes in July of 1921, Bertha eagerly undertook a determined walk along the path, which she felt would lead to fulfillment of her dreams of becoming a modern-day Florence Nightingale. She embraced her fellow classmates as sisters, and had many friendly interactions with members of the hospital staff, including Superintendent Lucy Pocock.



Early on, Bertha learned that her nursing education would involve not only extensive reading and studying, but would also give her many ‘hands-on’ experiences in practical nursing with real doctors and very authentic patients.
But, all was not nighttime assignments in operating rooms and emptying bedpans. The LDS Hospital was also a Mecca for many young men, desiring to date the attractive young student nurses residing there.

Bertha soon found herself attending church services, dances and movies with a succession of young men, who found her both attractive and exciting to squire to various events happening in Salt Lake City.

Moreover, there was a nearby United States Army post, located about three miles east of Dr. Grove’s LDS Hospital, where several hundred soldiers were stationed. Three years after the end of the great World War, Fort Douglas was home to a rather large contingent of peace-time soldiers, most of whom were single enlisted men.

It was not surprising, that many of these young men were often found, enjoying the activities and social life, which flourished in Salt Lake City.

Bertha was fond of dancing and going to the movies, and one evening, she met one of those young soldiers at The Auditorium--one of Salt Lake’s most popular dance halls. Staff Sergeant Clyde E. Weeks, recently having returned from a two-year assignment in the Philippine Islands, was still getting acquainted with the social life in Salt Lake City, when he spied Bertha Larsen on the dance floor, dancing with another man.

“What an attractive young lady,” he thought. “I would like to dance with her!”
The dark-eyed Canadian beauty had no problem accepting Clyde’s invitation to dance, and the merry-eyed soldier swept her into his arms and danced her around the floor, like Rudolph Valentino.
The date was Saturday, October 6, 1923.

AN AUSPICIOUS EVENING AT THE DANCE
Their meeting seemed to be rather unremarkable, for Bertha wrote in her diary for that date: “I went up to Farmer’s office. Mr. Scott was there, but I don’t think he likes me any more, but I still like him. I went down to Lulu’s place and got ready and went to The Auditorium at 9 o’clock. Met Clyde Weeks and he sure is cute. I came home with him. We came home with Ben Stevens.”

BERTHA’S DIARIES
For the first year and a half of Bertha’s sojourn at Dr. Grove’s LDS Hospital, she led a fascinating life, learning all about the mysteries about the human body and how caring and knowledgeable professional caregivers can relieve the distress of ill and injured bodies, as well as facilitating the healing of those on whom physicians have plied their skills.

And all the while, she remained a faithful member of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, regularly attending her meetings in the local ward, as well as sessions of the General Conference of the Church, held in the Salt Lake Tabernacle.

On New Years Day, 1923, Bertha Weeks was both surprised and pleased to receive from the President of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints a small booklet, entitled “Up From The Hills--His Story” by N.C. Hanks.
Inscribed inside the front cover was inscribed: 1923 Bertha Larsen: It is with much pleasure that I present you with this copy of “Up from the Hills.” It is the inspirational, personal story of a Utah boy, whose faith and courage in overcoming extraordinary difficulties deeply appealed to my sympathy and admiration. I think his story is the most inspirational for young men and women I have ever read, with the exception of that of Helen Keller, and puts to shame those of us who have eyes and hands and become discouraged in the battle of life.

Hoping that you and your loved ones had a very enjoyable Christmas, and wishing you a bright and prosperous New Year.

Very sincerely yours,

HEBER J. GRANT



For the remainder of her life, Bertha Larsen treasured this small booklet. The personal story of N.C. Hanks had resonated, deeply with this orphaned girl from Sterling, Alberta, Canada, who had lost both of her parents in the great influenza epidemic that had plagued the world in 1917-1919. Early on, she had determined that she would succeed, and that she would make a difference in this world.

While she was in nurse’s training at Dr. Groves LDS Hospital, Bertha Larsen had many admirers. She was popular with the young men and enjoyed their company, while all the while she was open to considering the possibilities for a husband.

The men who dated Bertha soon learned that she had high standards in her associations with those of the opposite gender. Her two-year Diary, begun January 1, 1923, well chronicles Bertha’s day-to-day interactions with a number of men, whose associations she enjoyed.

However, when the name of Clyde Weeks first appears in her diary, we see a most remarkable change in her demeanor toward him, as well as to the rest of the men she had been dating. On October 11, 1923, Bertha wrote: “Haven’t eaten a thing all day and worried and worried myself to death. I hope I can get over it soon. I really believe I like Clyde a little bit or I would not feel so badly. Can’t wait till he calls tonight after 7 o’clock.”

October 17, 1923: “Well, Clyde came up and we went down to the Paramount to see ‘The Spanish Dancer’ and it sure was good. I was really glad to see Clyde. Really, Dear Diary, I believe I like him a lot.”

COURTING FOR OVER A YEAR
During the following year, there were many entries in Bertha’s diary, detailing Bertha’s dates with several other men, as well as frequent dates with Clyde Weeks. Complicating their relationship, however, was the fact that Clyde was not a member of the LDS Church. Because of that fact, at one point, she tried to break off their relationship.

However, she recalled later, “I suddenly had a peaceful feeling that things would work out well for us. And they did!”

On the anniversary of their first meeting, Bertha wrote: “October 6, 1924. Just one year ago to-night! Oh, Dear Diary, that was a happy night, cause I met Clyde. And I love him more than anything else in the world. He is so darn good, and I miss him so much. Clyde came up at 7:30 and I am so happy. I can’t get along without him. I know I’m not going to even try. He’s mine and no one can take him from me! I love Clyde!”

Bertha and Clyde’s romance was almost culminated on November 7, 1924: “Oh, it is just one year and 1 month since Clyde and I started to go together. Bless his heart; I love him, so much more than the whole wide world. I’m taking my afternoon off, today, so I better stop and get ready to leave.

Clyde came up at 7:30, and we got a ring and headed for Farmington. But when we got there, the mayor was not home, and so we had to come back without getting hitched. Oh dear! More Fun!”

A MARRIAGE MADE IN HEAVEN
Bertha’s diary page for November 8, 1924 reads: “Oh dear, this morning we are going to do the deed! I came down to the office at 8:50, but Clyde didn’t call; till 9:30. I met him and we went up to the County building, and were married at about 10:05. Oh Boy! All my life is changed. I’m a new girl! I’m so happy…cause Clyde is all my own now. We are not going to tell anyone, since nursing students are not supposed to get married!”

However, Salt Lake County’s report of current marriages came out in the newspaper on November 12, and it did not take long, before nearly everyone who knew Bertha, had heard about her marriage to Clyde Weeks.

Two days later, Clyde and Bertha moved into the small apartment they had discovered near the hospital. Bertha’s diary recalled: “Went out on my noon hour today and got towels and sheets, etc. etc. and I am just thrilled to death. It’s going to be so much fun getting started and everything. I couldn’t wait till 5:30 arrived, hardly. I left at 5:00 and headed home to my hubby. Bless his heart. I love him so much, and we are going to be just awfully happy. We had the best little dinner! Oh! It seems so good to have Clyde home in the evening. I love him so much!”

Needless to say, spring rolled around. Bertha passed her Board Examinations, and on May 27, 1924, Graduation Exercises of the Dr. W.H. Groves Latter-day Saints Hospital School of Nursing were held in the Assembly Hall on Temple Square.

The twenty-five nursing graduates of the School of Nursing, including Bertha Larsen Weeks and her good friend, Dorothy Tuttle of Manti, Utah, received their white nursing uniform caps, denoting their new status as graduates and registered nurses.

HONEYMOON IN THE ORIENT
Meanwhile, a honeymoon in the Orient became a possibility for Clyde and Bertha Weeks. Clyde had described to Bertha his recent Army assignment in the Philippine Islands, from where he had departed with glowing recommendations from his superiors. He asked Bertha if she would be interested in going back with him to The Philippines on another duty assignment. Bertha was excited at the possibility of foreign travel and quickly agreed to his proposal.

A quick exchange of cablegrams between Fort Douglas and Manila resulted in the approval of Sergeant Weeks’ transfer back to Manila. Clyde and Bertha packed their bags and on September 25, 1925, they boarded the train for San Francisco, where they found the U.S. Thomas anchored in the bay, waiting to be their home for the long voyage across the Pacific.

Their journey together across the Pacific Ocean proved to be a relaxing and enjoyable honeymoon, as Clyde recounted to Bertha the details of his first voyage across the Pacific in 1918, during the Great War, which saw him serve his first Army years in Vladivostok, Russia.

A happy circumstance on their voyage was that Bertha was pregnant. Arriving in the Philippine Islands, Clyde and Bertha were assigned to occupy very pleasant housing on the wall of the old walled city of Cuartel-de-Espania in Manila.
On November 18, 1925, Clyde E. Weeks, Jr. was born in the Sternberg General Hospital in Manila. As a registered nurse, herself, the birth of her first child was not the first birth in which Bertha had participated. And it would not be the last.
From this point on, the lives of Bertha Larsen and Clyde E. Weeks were wedded eternally.

Condensed History

of

Bertha Margarete Larsen Weeks

 

Bertha Larsen Weeks was born in Salt Lake City, Utah in 1903. She lived with her parents and five brothers in central Utah until her teens. In 1920, she trained and found employment as a Registered Nurse at the LDS Hospital, in Salt Lake City. During that period, the U. S. Army had an active military post at Fort Douglas, in Salt Lake City. During that period, Bertha met an Army soldier named Clyde E. Weeks who was stationed at Fort Douglas. After appropriate courting, they fell in love and were married.

 

Clyde was soon stationed in the Philippine Islands. During that stay in the Philippines, Bertha gave birth to her first child, Clyde E. Weeks, Jr. Clyde and Bertha and Clyde Jr. eventually returned to the mainland and were again stationed at Fort Douglas. During this second stay at Fort Douglas, Bertha gave birth to her daughter, Sharee Margarete Weeks, and eight years later her second son, Michael Wayne Weeks was born.

 

During Bertha's final pregnancy, she was diagnosed with breast cancer. She received the best care available at the time, but she passed away from her illness in 1941, at the age of 36. Bertha had carried Michael during her pregnancy. Michael was only three years old when his mother died. Bertha was buried at this spot in Fort Douglas in 1941. Her husband and children continued to live in Utah after her death, and all fulfilled a normal life.
