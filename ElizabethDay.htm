HISTORY OF THOMAS GAMMON AND ELIZABETH DAY
At the time of Thomas Gammon's birth, George III was king of England (1760-1820), and James Monroe was President of the United States. England suffered from the worst agricultural conditions after the Battles of Trafalgar and Waterloo in the Napoleonic War. The government was deeply in debt; food prices rose sharply but not wages; there were higher taxes; farm prices fell. Farm workers such as the Gammons were starving.

George IV (1830-1837) during whose reign The Great Reform Bill of 1831 was the greatest step towards democracy in England in nearly 600 years. It was an opportune time for the Mormon missionaries who came to England in 1837, for the people were hungry and dissatisfied with their lives and the Church had so much to offer them.

Thomas was born 20 Jun 1819 and christened 27 Jun 1819 in Braunton, Devon. However, he always gave his birth date as 27 Jun 1820, and it was recorded as such in all the records found of him after he came to Utah -- Provo Ward Records, records kept by the family, obituary, tombstone, etc.

He married Elizabeth (Betsey) Day 27 Oct 1840. She was born 10 Apr 1817, Petrockstow, Devon, the daughter of Henry Day and Elizabeth. They were an aristocratic and wealthy family. Thomas and Elizabeth met when Thomas came to work for Mr. Day as a gardener. The parents felt their daughter was too good to marry a farm boy and told Betsey she would lose her inheritance if she married Thomas. This did not change their minds as they loved each other and eloped. Thomas could work no longer on the Day Estate, and so the young couple had to leave. Just where they lived the first years after their marriage is not known.

Charles, their first child, was born in Devonshire 27 Apr 1842. They were living in Neath, Glamorgan, Wales, at the time their second son, William, was born 26 Jul 1845. They were also living in Wales when they joined the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints; Thomas was baptized 21 Dec 1847 and Elizabeth a year later on 15 Dec 1848. The first LDS Conference was created in Wales in 1844.

The headquarters of the British Mission was in Liverpool, and it was the place of embarkation of nearly all the Latter-day Saints leaving for America--not only the British but the Scandinavians and other Europeans. Emigrants gathered by the thousands with their own bedding and equipment, including a two-gallon water can for every person, a teapot, a canister for tea and sugar, a frying pan, an iron pot for cooking, plates, cups and bowls, cutlery, and a chamber pot with a cover. Everyone in steerage was expected to do his own food preparation.

There were better accommodations for an extra ten shillings or a pound for those who could afford it. For considerably more money there were staterooms, a spacious living room and abundant food at high prices, with service either from the ship's employees or from emigrant girls eager to earn a little money. The great majority of the Saints traveled in steerage.

Thomas Gammon and Elizabeth left Liverpool, England, 18 Feb 1850 on the ship "Josiah Bradlee"; there were 263 passengers ("Crossing the Ocean 1849-1855" 38335 part 3). Charles had died prior to this time as he was not listed on the shipping lists. William was almost five years old. According to shipping lists in the Church Historical Department, the ship arrived in New Orleans 18 Apr 1850, a journey of 59 days.

C. Mansfield was the Master of the ship and T. Day was listed as the company leader. Elder Day writes: "The cooking seemed to try our patience most, but according to the manner in which our company was organized, accompanied with the diligence of the presidents of each section, order and peace prevailed, and the whole company could have tea and be on deck by 6 o'clock when the songs of Zion were sung more or less, which caused cheerfulness to beam on every countenance. Thus were our evenings generally passed until the signal was given for prayers, which were attended to by the presidents of each section at 8 o'clock in the morning and at the same hour in the evening, after which preparations were made for rest. . . There was a church meeting every Sunday, and everyone was given the chance of expressing their feelings about anything they wished."

After leaving the "Josiah Bradlee" in New Orleans, the passengers took another boat up the Mississippi and, just north of St. Louis, went west on the Missouri River to Council Bluffs, Iowa, where they remained two years before starting the final leg of their journey to Utah. According to Emma Percia Bunnell Blake, a grand-daughter, her grandparents reached Council Bluffs in May 1850 and remained there one year. There they acquired two yoke of oxen and four cows and these made their team while crossing the plains.

They moved from Council Bluffs to Newark, Missouri, where they lived before starting for Utah. They arrived in Provo, Utah, 16 Aug 1853.

Thomas and Elizabeth Gammon brought with them across the plains several window panes, which they used in the home they built later, an adobe mold, a tin lantern, and several pieces of beautiful handwork and things Elizabeth had made. It was said that she liked to sew nice pretty articles but did not mend overalls. Elizabeth also brought her beloved organ; apparently her parents let her take it from the family home. It was a treadle organ and a lovely piece of furniture. She and her daughter Mary Elizabeth, learned to play it when children. She gave it to her son, William, when he got married; and his eldest daughter, Elon, learned to play the organ when she was a child. It was in William's home at the time of his death.

Their first home in Provo was the covered wagon in which they crossed the plains. It was while living here that their last two children were born--Henry Franklin 30 Dec 1854 and Mary Elizabeth 22 Aug 1857, Henry died before the 1860 census was taken, but no date of his death has been found.

It was slow, tedious work to build their home, as Thomas used the adobe mold he brought with him to Utah and he could make only two bricks at a time. His house was the first one in Provo to have glass windows. How they must have looked forward to having a nice, comfortable home.

During their early life in Provo the Church started developing cooperatives with the aim of enabling the people to be their own merchants and share in the profits of business and also to supply their wants. It was boasted that with the event of the railroad Mormonism would be overtaken and Brigham Young would be de-throned and the "old settlers" treated without consideration. Another reason was that individuals had no capital to enter business themselves; many persons with small means united in a cooperative plan.

Connected with this movement was an extension of the effort to restrict Latter-day Saint trading to those of their own confraternity. In the boycott of 1865-66 a distinction had been made between non-Mormon merchants who were hostile to the Latter-day Saints and had joined forces with the anti-Mormon political agitators. But at the October Conference of 1868 this distinction was no longer adhered to; a boycott was urged against all non-Mormons. Steps were taken to make the resolution that the Latter-day Saints be a self-sustaining people. Those inclined to act independently and to trade where they pleased were warned that they could not hope to retain fellowship with the Latter-day Saints if they persisted in that course. Elizabeth Gammon bought sewing materials from a non-Mormon merchant. They were well pleased with the service they received and were treated fairly by the merchant. She continued to buy materials there because she resented the boycott.

When they continued to patronize the store, she and her husband were disfellowshipped 10 Mar 1869 "for apostasy in favor of Josephitism" (Provo 2nd Ward Records). William and Charlotte Gammon were also disfellowshipped 19 Dec 1869 "apostasy in favor of Godbe and Harrison". Thomas and Elizabeth became bitter toward the Church, but they were later reinstated and remained active.

Thomas Gammon was a polygamist. He was sealed to Sarah Ann Brown 13 Mar 1856 by George A. Smith in Provo, Utah in the G. A. Smith house. According to this record she was born Oct 1826, Cooms Morr, Herefordshire, England. The Provo 2nd Ward Records (Film 026324) states she was born 27 Oct 1827, England, the daughter of Thomas Brown and Ann. Thomas and Sarah Ann had one daughter, Mattie, born 4 Apr 1859, Provo, Utah. "This marriage did not set well with Grandma, so she told Grandpa he could have his choice but she would not live in polygamy. The mother (Sarah Ann) did not like polygamy either so she left Grandpa and went to Missouri, taking her little girl with her. She joined the Josephites and went back to the headquarters of the Josephites in Independence, Missouri (Reorganized Church)." (Emma Percia Bunnell Blake) Sarah Ann married second Henry Kemp, and Mattie was known throughout her life as Mattie Kemp. Mattie married William D. Roberts born Aug 1856 in Iowa, and they had four children all born in Independence, Mo.: Eva A., Earl D., Stanley S., and Ruby C. (This is the daughter and four grandchildren mentioned in Thomas' obituary.)

Thomas Gammon died 27 Jan 1897 and is buried in the Provo City Cemetery. Following is a copy of his obituary:

Thomas Gammon, an old resident of Provo 2nd Ward, died peacefully after a lingering illness, Wednesday at 20 minutes to 2 p.m. Jan. 27, 1897. Deceased was born June 27, 1820 in Brampton, Devonshire, England. He was raised a farmer, married Elizabeth Day 27 Oct 1840 and moved to Neath in Wales shortly after. He and his wife joined the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints in 1848, and two years later commenced their journey across the great ocean for Utah. It took eight weeks and four days to cross the Atlantic. They reached Council Bluffs, Iowa, in May 1850, where they remained one year, afterwards moving to Newark, Missouri, where they lived two years before crossing the plains with ox teams to Utah. He arrived in Provo Sept 18, 1853. Since that time he has continued to reside here, enduring many of the hardships of frontier life. Mr. Gammon was an honest, industrious, and peaceable man, a good neighbor, husband and father. He was the eldest of four brothers and three sisters. He leaves a wife, one son, two daughters, eighteen grandchildren, two great-grandchildren, and a host of friends. All his family live here except one daughter and four grandchildren who live in Independence, Missouri.

Elizabeth Day Gammon's obituary in the Provo newspaper states that she (better known as Betsey) "died on February 23, 1899 at the home of her daughter, Mary Elizabeth Bunnell. She was almost 82 years old. She died of general disabilities. She and her late husband, Thomas Gammon, had lived in the 2nd Ward in Provo most of the time since they arrived there in 1853. The funeral is Saturday February 25, 1899 at 1 PM at the Second Ward Meeting House." She also was buried in the Provo City Cemetery. Thomas was known as a genial, easy-going man and a hard worker. Elizabeth was very neat and clean and a perfectionist in all she did. She always called her husband "Gammon". She had black hair and very blue eyes and was tall and thin.
